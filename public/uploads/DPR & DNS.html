AGRICULTURE

Farmers could receive up to €7,000 in aid from EU funds – Peter Agius

[Malta, AGRICULTURE]

km-DNS-MT Monday, July 6, 2020 8:14:00 AM CEST

The Malta Independent reported today that former PN MEP candidate Peter Agius said yesterday that farmers could receive up to €7,000 in aid from European Union funds after the European Council adopted a special regulation which allows Member States, as an exceptional measure, to pay up to €7,000 to farmers and up to €50,000 to small and medium enterprises (SME’s) active in processing, marketing or development of agricultural products or cotton, except fishery products. Agius said that the aim was to make use of available funds under existing rural development programmes to provide support to farmers and SME’s worst-hit by the COVID-19 crisis and to address the liquidity and cash-flow problems stemming from the closures of shops, markets and restaurants.

EMPLOYMENT

Malta with largest decrease in its Job Vacancy Rate among EU Member States

[Malta, EMPLOYMENT]

km-DNS-MT Monday, July 6, 2020 8:16:00 AM CEST

The Malta Independent today reported on Eurostat statistics which show that Malta, along with Italy and Latvia, experienced the largest decrease in the job vacancy rate in the EU. During the first quarter of 2020, the job vacancy rate in the euro area was 1.9%, down from 2.2% in the previous quarter, and from 2.3% in the first quarter of 2019. The job vacancy rate in the EU was also 1.9% in the first quarter of 2020, down from 2.1% in the previous quarter, and from 2.3% in the first quarter of 2019. The highest job vacancy rates in the first quarter of 2020 were recorded in Czechia, Belgium and Austria, whilst the lowest rates were seen in Poland, Italy, Ireland and Bulgaria.

HEALTH

Zero new COVID-19 patients, 12 active cases remaining

[Malta, HEALTH]

km-DNS-MT Monday, July 6, 2020 8:19:00 AM CEST

Times of Malta said that the health authorities said that no new COVID-19 cases were reported yesterday. Malta has now reached the milestone of 100,000 tests for the novel coronavirus and these includes repeat tests, meaning the same individuals might have been swabbed more than once. The authorities said yesterday that, with another patient having recovered, there were now 11 remaining active COVID-19 cases. Superintendent of Public Health Charmaine Gauci said that, from the start, the authorities had a strong testing strategy to suppress the resurgence of local outbreaks by quickly identifying cases, isolating them and identifying contacts. Local media last Saturday reported that only one new case of Coronavirus was registered between Thursday and Friday, while one patient has recovered. Just days after the reopening of the airport, the Health Authorities said that the case registered was an imported, sporadic, symptomatic case. On Sunday the media reported that there were no new cases of COVID-19 identified between Friday and Saturday night, according to data released by the Health Ministry on Saturday.

Maltese have highest trust in health system

[Malta, HEALTH]

km-DNS-MT Monday, July 6, 2020 8:18:00 AM CEST

Malta Today reported that, according to an EU-wide online survey by Eurofound, Malta and Denmark had the highest trust in their national healthcare system (8.1 and 8.0 out of a maximum of 10 respectively) in all 27 EU Member States, and Hungary had the lowest (3.9). Trust in the EU in Malta was also slightly higher than the EU27 average, at 5 out of 10. Malta also had the fourth best score when it comes to the mental health index. Moreover, nearly 60% of Maltese remain optimistic about their future. However, nearly 45% reported their financial situation to be worse than three months ago. The survey also paints a stark picture of people across the EU who have seen their economic situation worsen and are deeply concerned about their financial future.

JUSTICE

Passport buyers must first live in Malta for a year

[Malta, JUSTICE]

km-DNS-MT Monday, July 6, 2020 8:25:00 AM CEST

Times of Malta, The Malta Independent and L-Orizzont reported last Saturday that last Friday Citizenship Parliamentary Secretary Alex Muscat announced new regulations to Malta’s Individual Investor Programme scheme. Through the new regulations as from September anyone interested in getting a Maltese passport will soon have to live in Malta for a year before being allowed to apply for citizenship. Under the new scheme, applicants who invest €750,000, up from €650,000 under the existing rules, will be able to apply for citizenship after living in Malta for one year. Applicants who invest €600,000 will have to spend three years in Malta before applying for a passport. The minimum value of the property purchased will double to €700,000 from €350,000. A €10,000 philanthropic donation will be mandatory. Up to 400 citizenship applications will be approved per year, with the scheme capped at a maximum of 1,500.

SOCIAL AFFAIRS, SKILLS AND LABOUR MOBILITY

EU funding for active citizenship

[Malta, SOCIAL_AFFAIRS]

km-DNS-MT Sunday, July 5, 2020 11:08:00 AM CEST

Parliamentary Secretary for European Funds, Stefan Zrinzo Azzopardi, wrote in The Sunday Times of Malta yesterday on the importance and need of EU funding for active citizenship. Zrinzo Azzopardi calls on youths to participate in two new schemes launched for voluntary organisations and members of the Malta Council for Economic and Social Development (MCESD), for which €2 million of EU funds have been made available. TAXATION

Pay it black! Maltese most likely to buy goods and services without VAT

[Malta, TAXATION]

km-DNS-MT Sunday, July 5, 2020 11:10:00 AM CEST

Malta Today yesterday reported that a Eurobarometer survey shows that the Maltese are the most likely in Europe to say that they have bought undeclared goods or services. Malta recorded 30% of respondents saying that they had acquired goods or services which they believed were ‘undeclared’ because no receipt or invoice was handed to them, whilst the EU average was only 10%. The highest proportion were reported in Malta, the Netherlands and Greece, whilst the lowest percentage was reported in Poland, the UK and Germany. 52% of the Maltese replied that they had only realised that the goods or services were undeclared after paying. Only 30% replied that they had done so because undeclared goods and services are cheaper. Meanwhile 74% of Maltese said that they considered tax evasion by private firms as ‘absolutely unacceptable’, well above the EU average of 54%.
